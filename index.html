The House

A quiet edge between the cedar and the sea.

The Beach House sits where the land softens into the Salish Sea — a low-slung retreat held between old-growth canopy and tidal rhythm. Mornings are thresholds here; the kind that begin with mist on the rocks and end with herons against the channel.

Designed for slow gatherings, deep conversation, and the easy weight of being together near the water.

What sets it apart

Three things you'll notice first.

— 01

Closer to the water

Steps from the shoreline, not a hike. The tide is part of the floor plan — close enough to hear at every meal, every conversation, every quiet morning.

— 02

Unobstructed views

An open western horizon. No neighboring rooflines, no power lines, no compromise — just channel, islands, and the long arc of the evening light.

— 03

Room to gather

A wide communal entry holds the whole arrival — coats, conversation, a long table, room for everyone. The house begins where people meet.

Inside the house

A space that holds the day.

From the entry hall to the deck above the rocks, every room turns toward the water.

Orcas Island, Washington

Twenty miles off the mainland — a different clock entirely.

Reached by ferry through the San Juans, Orcas is the largest of the islands and the quietest in tone. Old forests, slow roads, working harbors. Eagles in the cedars. The kind of place that asks you to stay an extra night.

The Beach House is on the western shore, where the water is widest and the sunsets last longest.
